When Foundation Model Meets Federated Learning: Motivations, Challenges, and Future Directions
arXiv 2306.15546
When Foundation Model Meets Federated Learning:
Motivations, Challenges, and Future Directions
Jianbing Ni · Zhipeng Cai · Kuan-Ching Li · Laurence T. Yang
IEEE Senior Members
Abstract

Foundation models (FMs) are general-purpose AI models that can be adapted to a wide range of downstream tasks. On the other hand, federated learning (FL) is a distributed machine learning paradigm that aims to train models collaboratively while preserving data privacy. The integration of foundation models and federated learning presents both exciting opportunities and significant challenges.

In this paper, we first discuss the motivations for combining foundation models with federated learning, exploring how FL can leverage the powerful representations learned by FMs while simultaneously enabling privacy-preserving distributed training across heterogeneous data silos. We then systematically analyze the key challenges, including communication efficiency, heterogeneity handling, and privacy concerns. Finally, we outline promising future research directions in this emerging intersection.

1. Introduction

The rapid advancement of large-scale foundation models, such as GPT-4, LLaMA, and CLIP, has revolutionized artificial intelligence. These models, trained on massive curated datasets, have demonstrated remarkable zero-shot and few-shot generalization across diverse tasks. However, their training requires vast amounts of data that are often distributed across multiple organizations or devices, each subject to strict privacy regulations.

Federated learning (FL) has emerged as a principled solution to this challenge by enabling collaborative model training without centralizing raw data. In conventional FL, participants train local models on their private data and share only model updates—gradients or weights—with a central aggregator. This paradigm has been successfully applied in healthcare, finance, and on-device intelligence.

The convergence of foundation models and federated learning represents a natural and compelling frontier. FMs can serve as powerful backbones that are fine-tuned in a federated manner, allowing diverse participants to collaboratively improve models without compromising data privacy. Conversely, FL methods can be enhanced by the rich semantic representations provided by FMs.

2. Motivations

Several compelling motivations drive the study of this intersection. First, data privacy and regulatory compliance: Many real-world datasets are governed by regulations such as GDPR and HIPAA, prohibiting centralized aggregation. FL provides a principled framework for training FMs on distributed private data.

Second, data heterogeneity: Data across federated clients exhibits significant statistical heterogeneity—varying distributions, label spaces, and feature modalities. The rich representational capacity of FMs may help mitigate the negative impact of such heterogeneity in federated settings.

3. Key Challenges

Despite the opportunities, integrating FMs with FL introduces several critical challenges that must be addressed to realize practical systems.

Communication Efficiency. Foundation models typically have billions of parameters, making communication of full model updates prohibitively expensive. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods—LoRA, adapter layers, prefix tuning—become essential in this context to reduce the bandwidth footprint.

System Heterogeneity. Federated participants operate under vastly different computational constraints. Running LLM inference and fine-tuning on edge devices requires model compression, quantization, and adaptive scheduling strategies that are largely unexplored in the FM-FL setting.

Privacy and Security. While FL inherently limits raw data exposure, model updates can still leak sensitive information through gradient inversion attacks. Composing FL with formal differential privacy guarantees is critical yet challenging, as large model gradients have high sensitivity.

Catastrophic Forgetting. When FMs are continually fine-tuned across federated rounds, they risk forgetting previously acquired knowledge. Federated continual learning strategies are needed to maintain both general and task-specific capabilities.

4. Future Directions

We identify several high-priority research directions. Federated PEFT remains an open problem: existing PEFT methods were not designed under federated constraints, and developing new methods that minimize communication while preserving model quality is urgent.

Personalized FM fine-tuning is another promising direction, where each client learns a personalized adapter on top of a shared FM backbone, balancing global knowledge with local specialization.

Finally, federated foundation models for multimodal tasks—spanning vision, language, and sensor data—present both new challenges and opportunities. Cross-modal federated alignment methods will be central to this effort.

5. Conclusion

In this paper, we have systematically examined the intersection of foundation models and federated learning. We discussed motivations rooted in data privacy and heterogeneity, analyzed key challenges across communication, system constraints, and privacy, and outlined future research directions. We hope this work serves as a useful reference for researchers and practitioners working at this exciting frontier.
